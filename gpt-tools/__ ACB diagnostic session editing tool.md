# 🧩 Teaching Overview: Understanding ACBs (Assumption → Conclusion → Behavior)

### **Purpose**

In Break Method, the ACB framework maps the predictable internal logic that drives every recurring emotional or behavioral pattern.  
It helps practitioners identify where perception becomes distortion — and how that distortion limits available choices in the present moment.

* * *

## 1. **A = Assumption**

### **Definition**

An **Assumption** is a subconscious rule formed in early childhood through sensory and emotional learning.  
Because the developing brain prioritizes _efficiency over accuracy_, it fills in missing information and labels experiences through distorted logic.  
These original rules still operate in adulthood — the brain applies old rules to new input, filtering perception through self-deception.

### **Key Points**

* Based on sensory and emotional impressions, not logic.
    
* Acts as the **lens of distortion** that interprets new experiences.
    
* Feels like an unshakable truth to the client (“People always leave,” “Nothing is safe,” “I have to be perfect”).
    
* The practitioner’s job is to identify the rule — **not debate its validity.**
    

* * *

## 2. **C = Conclusion**

### **Definition**

A **Conclusion** is the mental decision or rule that forms when a current stimulus passes through the Assumption.  
It’s the language architecture of choice — the statement the brain writes about what must happen next to stay safe or in control.

**Example:**  
Stimulus: Mom smells like alcohol.  
Assumption: Mom drinks when things are unsafe.  
Conclusion: “Mom isn’t safe to confide in when she’s been drinking.”

### **Key Points**

* The Conclusion defines what the person believes they _must_ do next.
    
* It’s context-specific but pattern-consistent.
    
* It filters out alternative actions, narrowing perceived choices.
    
* The practitioner’s goal is to locate the **exact rule sentence** the client lives by in that moment (“I have to fix this,” “I can’t trust them,” “I shouldn’t speak up”).
    

* * *

## 3. **B = Behavior**

### **Definition**

The **Behavior** is the visible output of the distorted logic chain.  
It’s the automatic action or inaction that occurs once the Conclusion has been reached.  
Because the Assumption distorts input and the Conclusion limits perceived options, the Behavior feels like the _only logical response_ — not a choice.

### **Key Points**

* Behavior is driven, not chosen — the system perceives it as the safest or only option.
    
* It reinforces the original Assumption, completing the loop.
    
* Examples: micromanaging, withdrawing, people-pleasing, procrastinating, self-criticism.
    
* The practitioner’s goal is to observe how each behavior proves the Assumption true, keeping the loop intact.

# 🧩 Teaching Overview: Understanding ACBs (Assumption → Conclusion → Behavior)

### **Purpose**

In Break Method, the ACB framework maps the predictable internal logic that drives every recurring emotional or behavioral pattern.  
It helps practitioners identify where perception becomes distortion — and how that distortion limits available choices in the present moment.

* * *

## 1. **A = Assumption**

An **Assumption** is a subconscious rule formed in early childhood through sensory and emotional learning.  
Because the developing brain prioritizes _efficiency over accuracy_, it fills in missing information and labels experiences through distorted logic.  
These original rules still operate in adulthood — the brain applies old rules to new input, filtering perception through self-deception.

### **Key Points**

* Based on sensory and emotional impressions, not logic.
    
* Acts as the **lens of distortion** that interprets new experiences.
    
* Feels like an unshakable truth to the client (“People always leave,” “Nothing is safe,” “I have to be perfect”).
    
* The practitioner’s job is to identify the rule — **not debate its validity.**
    

* * *

## 2. **C = Conclusion**

A **Conclusion** is the mental rule that forms when a current stimulus passes through the Assumption.  
It defines **what must happen next** for the person to feel safe or in control.  
This is where internal logic turns into instruction.

### **Key Points**

* The Conclusion defines what the person believes they _must_ do next.
    
* It’s context-specific but pattern-consistent.
    
* It filters out alternative actions, narrowing perceived choices.
    
* The practitioner’s goal is to locate the **exact rule sentence** the client lives by in that moment (“I have to fix this,” “I can’t trust them,” “I shouldn’t speak up”).
    

* * *

## 3. **B = Behavior**

The **Behavior** is the visible output of the distorted logic chain — the automatic action or inaction that occurs once the Conclusion has been reached.  
Because the Assumption distorts input and the Conclusion limits perceived options, the Behavior feels like the _only logical response_ — not a conscious choice.

### **Key Points**

* Behavior is driven, not chosen.
    
* It reinforces the original Assumption, completing the loop.
    
* Examples: micromanaging, withdrawing, people-pleasing, procrastinating, self-criticism.
    
* The practitioner’s job is to observe how each behavior proves the Assumption true, keeping the loop intact.

# **ACB Framework: Assumption → Conclusion → Behavior**

### **Purpose**

The ACB framework outlines the internal logic sequence that governs recurring emotional and behavioral patterns.  
It enables practitioners to locate the point at which perception becomes distortion and behavior becomes automatic rather than chosen.

* * *

## **A = Assumption**

An **Assumption** is a subconscious rule formed in early childhood through sensory and emotional learning.  
Because the developing brain favors efficiency over accuracy, it fills in missing information and labels experiences using distorted logic.  
These rules continue to filter perception throughout adulthood, creating a consistent bias in how experiences are interpreted.

### **Key Points**

* Based on emotional and sensory impressions, not reasoning.
    
* Functions as a fixed perceptual lens that interprets current events.
    
* Experienced by the client as an unshakable truth.
    
* The practitioner’s task is to identify the rule, not challenge its validity.
    

* * *

## **C = Conclusion**

A **Conclusion** is the internal directive that forms when new input passes through the Assumption.  
It dictates what the individual believes must occur next to remain safe or in control.

### **Key Points**

* Converts perception into behavioral instruction.
    
* Narrows the range of perceived choices.
    
* Remains consistent across different contexts.
    
* Practitioners aim to capture the precise internal phrasing (“I have to…,” “I can’t…,” “I shouldn’t…”).
    

* * *

## **B = Behavior**

**Behavior** represents the visible output of the distorted logic sequence.  
It occurs automatically once the Conclusion has been reached and is perceived by the client as the only logical course of action.

### **Key Points**

* Feels necessary or protective to the client.
    
* Reinforces the original Assumption, completing the self-confirming loop.
    
* Common forms include control, withdrawal, appeasement, perfectionism, or avoidance.
    
* Practitioner observation focuses on how behavior sustains the distortion.

# **Switch Control Patterns**

### **Overview**

The **Switch Control** presentation is an adaptive variant of _Abandonment – Control to Be Safe_ in which the individual alternates between **overt** and **covert** control expressions.  
The motivation—maintaining safety through control—remains constant, but the _method_ changes depending on context, relationship, or perceived emotional risk.

A true Switch pattern is confirmed when **documented oscillations in behavior** mirror the **conditional logic** written into the ACBs.  
These oscillations may appear across environments, relationships, or phases of perceived security and must be represented in the Adaptive Conclusions with _if/then_ or _when/then_ phrasing.

* * *

## **Subtype 1: Contextual Switch**

**Definition:** Control expression shifts according to environment or role.  
**Mechanism:** Overt control in structured, performance-based settings; covert control in relational or emotionally charged settings.  
**Common Presentation:** Directive at work, conciliatory at home.  
**Verification:** Timeline shows environment-dependent oscillation.

**Example Conclusions**

* “When I’m at work, **I have to take charge**; when I’m at home, **I have to stay calm and agreeable.**”
    
* “In professional settings I manage directly; in emotional settings I manage quietly.”
    

* * *

## **Subtype 2: Relational Switch**

**Definition:** Control expression varies with relational closeness or emotional risk.  
**Mechanism:** Overt control with low-risk or less-intimate relationships; covert control with high-stakes or emotionally close relationships.  
**Common Presentation:** Directive with colleagues, deferential with partner or parent.  
**Verification:** Timeline shows person-specific oscillation linked to relational proximity.

**Example Conclusions**

* “With people I care about, **I have to tread lightly**; with others, **I can be direct.**”
    
* “I keep the peace with loved ones but take charge elsewhere.”
    

* * *

## **Subtype 3: Security-Based Switch**

**Definition:** Control expression shifts once safety or stability is established.  
**Mechanism:** Covert early to secure safety, overt later once security is perceived.  
**Common Presentation:** Appeasing early in relationships; directive once stability is achieved.  
**Verification:** Timeline shows time- or phase-based oscillation from appeasing → directive.

**Example Conclusions**

* “I have to stay agreeable until things feel stable; once they do, **I have to take over.**”
    
* “I hold back at first, then lead once it’s safe.”
    

* * *

## **Subtype 4: Reversed Security-Based Switch**

**Definition:** Control polarity runs opposite the typical pattern—**overt control within safe relationships** and **covert control in unfamiliar or evaluative settings.**  
**Mechanism:** Emotional safety lowers inhibition, allowing overt control; unfamiliar contexts activate appeasement to preserve acceptance.  
**Common Presentation:** Dominant with family or close partners; agreeable with new acquaintances or authority figures.  
**Verification:** Timeline shows relational-phase oscillation matching this direction.

**Example Conclusions**

* “With people I know and trust, **I have to take charge** so things are done right, but with new people, **I have to stay agreeable until I know where I stand.**”
    
* “I keep things smooth with strangers, but once I’m comfortable, **I step in and manage directly.**”

* Shifts in tone, assertiveness, and communication style.
    
* Alternation between dominance and appeasement within or across relationships.
    
* Self-description emphasizing adaptability or diplomacy.
    
* Justification framed as “reading the room” or “adjusting to people.”

# **Section: Conducting an ACB Diagnostic Session**

* * *

## **Purpose**

The **ACB (Assumption → Conclusion → Behavior)** diagnostic session trains the practitioner to identify the internal logic system that drives a client’s repetitive behavioral and emotional patterns.  
By mapping each layer of the sequence, practitioners can locate the precise point at which perception becomes distortion and behavior becomes automatic rather than chosen.

This process forms the foundation of all subsequent rewiring and ELI (Emotion + Logic Interrupt) interventions.

* * *

## **Session Overview**

### **Client Preparation**

* Prior to the session, the client watches _Dismantling Systems of Language_ and drafts their initial ACBs.
    
* The drafts are self-reported and often contain accurate insights but are typically out of sequence or contain blurred distinctions between Origin and Adaptive layers.
    

### **Practitioner Role**

* Act as a real-time **editor and coder**, refining client drafts so that each ACB accurately reflects developmental order and internal logic.
    
* Conduct the session with the computer screen visible to the client so revisions are collaborative and transparent.
    
* Maintain curiosity and neutrality—clients should experience discovery, not correction.
    

* * *

## **Session Objectives**

1. Identify and verify the **Origin Source Belief ACB** (early childhood assumption and conclusion).
    
2. Identify and verify the **Adaptive Source Belief ACB** (later compensatory logic).
    
3. Calibrate all behaviors against the **Timeline Imbalance** to ensure consistency.
    
4. Confirm that the baton-pass from Origin → Adaptive reads as one continuous, logical progression.
    

* * *

## **ACB Framework**

| Level | Definition | Practitioner Focus |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Assumption (A)** | A global, subconscious rule about people or the world formed through emotional learning between ages 2-5. | Phrase as “People are…” or “People will…” |
| **Conclusion (C)** | The automatic rule or directive that tells the client what must happen next to stay safe or in control. | Phrase as “I have to…,” “I need to…,” “I can’t…,” “I shouldn’t…” |
| **Behavior (B)** | The visible output of the internal logic chain; the action or inaction that feels inevitable. | Verify that behavior aligns with both ACB logic and Timeline data. |

* * *

## **Practitioner Process**

### **1. Review Client Drafts**

* Begin by reading each ACB aloud exactly as written.
    
* Ask clarifying questions only when logic or order is unclear.
    
* Avoid paraphrasing in early stages—preserve client language for accuracy.
    

### **2. Sequence Verification**

* Ensure developmental order:
    
    * **Origin Layer:** global rule about others → child’s safety strategy.
        
    * **Adaptive Layer:** refined adult logic → current behavior.
        
* Correct any inversion (e.g., if client writes self-referential statements as Assumptions).
    

### **3. Timeline Congruence Check**

* Cross-reference ACBs with the client’s **Timeline Diagnostics** to confirm polarity and oscillation patterns.
    
* The ACB and Timeline must describe the same behavioral reality; if not, one requires revision.
    

### **4. Edit for Structural Accuracy**

Use this standard:

| Common Client Issue | Correction Strategy |
| --- | --- |
| **Accurate idea, wrong order** | Reorder statements; identify which belief belongs to childhood (Origin) and which to adaptive logic. |
| **Blurring Origin / Adaptive** | Clarify with: “Would a child think this way, or is this what you learned later to manage people and outcomes?” |
| **Missing intermediate logic** | Ask: “If that’s what people are like, what do you decide you have to do about it at that age?” |
| **Emotional or vague phrasing** | Convert to rule-based language. Avoid “feel,” “hope,” or “want.” |
| **Contradiction with Timeline** | Determine whether Timeline or ACB language requires recalibration. Both must tell the same story. |

### **Purpose**

To define the foundational distortion through which the client perceives safety and threat in the world.

### **Language Architecture**

* Always global, always about others.
    
* Begin with _“People are…”_ or _“People will…”_.
    
* Express as absolute, not situational, truth.

**Behavioral Verification:**  
Behavior should reflect vigilance, control, or withdrawal that reinforces the original distortion.

### **Purpose**

To define the compensatory logic created between ages 6–12 that manages or controls the unsafe world established by the Origin layer.

### **Language Architecture**

* Begins with _“People are…”_ or _“People will…”_ but introduces rationalization.
    
* Reads as a management plan rather than a raw fear.
    
* Must flow directly from the Origin statement (the _baton-pass_ principle).
    

**Conclusion Rules**

* Begins with _“I have to…”_ / _“I need to…”_ / _“I can’t…”_ / _“I shouldn’t…”_.
    
* Describes specific, automatic management strategies.

## **Switch Control Patterns**

### **Overview**

Switch Control clients alternate between overt and covert expressions of control depending on context, relationship, or perceived safety.  
The underlying motive—maintaining safety through control—remains constant.

A true Switch pattern is confirmed when **documented oscillations in behavior** correspond to the **conditional logic** written into the ACBs.

### **Subtype Guide**

| Subtype | Mechanism | Verification | Example C (Conclusion) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Contextual Switch** | Changes style by environment or role. | Timeline shows environment-dependent oscillation. | “When I’m at work, I have to take charge; at home, I have to stay calm and agreeable.” |
| **Relational Switch** | Adjusts control by emotional closeness. | Timeline shows person-specific oscillation. | “With people I care about, I have to tread lightly; with others, I can be direct.” |
| **Security-Based Switch** | Begins covertly, becomes overt as security increases. | Timeline shows time-based oscillation (appeasing → directive). | “I have to stay agreeable until things feel stable; then I can take over.” |
| **Reversed Security-Based Switch** | Overt control in safe relationships, covert control in new or evaluative settings. | Timeline shows relational-phase oscillation matching overt ↔ covert direction. | “With people I trust, I have to take charge; with new people, I have to stay agreeable.” |

**Behavioral Correlates:**  
Tone and assertiveness fluctuate; client frames oscillation as adaptability or diplomacy.  
Self-description often includes _“I can be either type depending on the situation.”_

**Timeline Verification:**

* Oscillation must appear in behavioral data, not inferred from affect.
    
* Polarity direction (covert → overt or overt → covert) must match ACB conditional clauses.
    
* Category 5 / 6 variability may provide context but is not diagnostic.

1. Behavior described in ACBs appears consistently in real-world examples.
    
2. Tone and action level align with Timeline polarity (rigid, impulsive, flexible, etc.).
    
3. Oscillation patterns observed behaviorally mirror ACB conditional logic.
    
4. Inconsistencies are reconciled—either the ACB or Timeline is rewritten.

| Error | Description | Correction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Reversed Logic** | Self-referential A statements (“I can’t be myself”). | Flip order: A = rule about others; C = self-directed strategy. |
| **Mixed Pattern Language** | Abandonment logic in Rejection pattern (capability vs. judgment). | Re-anchor A around perception or blame for Rejection cases. |
| **Missing Baton-Pass** | Origin and Adaptive layers read as unrelated. | Rewrite so Adaptive picks up logically from Origin. |
| **Contradiction with Timeline** | Behavior in ACB conflicts with documented oscillation. | Determine which dataset is inaccurate; revise for congruence. |

# **Section: Emotional Addiction Cycle ACBs**

* * *

## **Purpose**

While **Source Belief ACBs** describe _relational rules_ that shape global interpersonal dynamics, **Emotional Addiction Cycle (EAC) ACBs** capture the _situational logic_ that unfolds within a single emotional sequence over time.

Each emotion has a **chronological pathway** consisting of three functional phases:

1. **Origin** – The first spark of emotional activation (the baseline trigger).
    
2. **Protective** – The compensatory response used to suppress, redirect, or rationalize the origin emotion.
    
3. **Escalating** – The behavioral amplification that sustains or re-creates the emotional state, completing the addictive loop.
    

Every phase has its own **Assumption → Conclusion → Behavior (ACB)** structure. Together, these ACBs create a self-reinforcing pattern that operates situationally—moment to moment—rather than globally like Source Beliefs.

* * *

## **Practitioner Orientation**

* Emotional Addiction Cycle ACBs are mapped **within context**, not personality type.
    
* The practitioner’s task is to **capture how the emotional state builds** inside a discrete situation (conflict, stress, anticipation, rejection, etc.).
    
* Each emotion must be tracked as it **progresses through the three ACB sets** in real time.
    
* Practitioners should record one complete ACB chain for each emotional phase before moving to the next.

### **Core Characteristics**

* Always fast, automatic, and pre-verbal.
    
* Based on stored historical associations between stimuli and perceived threat.
    
* Determines how quickly the Protective and Escalating phases are initiated.
    
* The language is **situational**, describing _this moment_ rather than the global world view.

| **Behavior (B)** | The immediate automatic reaction—physical, verbal, or cognitive.

# **3. Escalating Emotional ACBs**

## **Purpose**

The **Escalating Emotional ACB** captures the final stage of the Emotional Addiction Cycle — the collapse or explosion that occurs when the protective system can no longer maintain control.  
At this point, the emotional sequence shifts from _management_ to _confirmation_: the client experiences the very outcome they feared and interprets it as proof that their worldview is correct.

This phase produces the **emotional payoff** that reinforces the addictive pattern — a predictable sense of disappointment, failure, or futility that “completes the story” of the emotional cycle.

* * *

## **Core Function**

* **Origin:** anticipates instability (_“Something might go wrong”_).
    
* **Protective:** over-functions to prevent it (_“I have to manage this before it spirals”_).
    
* **Escalating:** confirms the inevitability of loss or failure (_“No matter what I do, it still falls apart”_).
    

At this point, the client’s system has exhausted its capacity to stay in regulation. The protective logic collapses into **resentment, defeat, or shame**, depending on the Timeline polarity.

# **IV. Practitioner Training: Independent ELI Construction**

### **Purpose**

This section outlines the cognitive and linguistic standards required for practitioners to design **ELI (Emotion + Logic Interrupt)** sequences independently.  
ELIs function as applied cognitive-engineering tools that expose contradictions between intent and outcome, challenge emotional justification, and facilitate behavioral re-patterning.  
Precision in both conceptual sequencing and language is essential to ensure that each ELI sequence activates higher-order reasoning rather than reinforcing emotional narrative.

* * *

## **1. Conceptual Integrity Before Language**

Practitioners must identify the **belief logic** before formulating questions.  
If the underlying Strongman logic (the internal justification sustaining the behavior) is unclear, the resulting sequence will lack depth and redundancy will occur.  
Concept precedes wording; the goal is to challenge the reasoning that maintains the pattern, not simply to describe the behavior itself.

* * *

## **2. Escalation Through Logical Progression**

An effective ELI sequence mirrors a gradual **descent into cognitive truth**.  
Each question should advance the client’s reasoning rather than restate the same concept.

To ensure progressive depth, vary the **function** of questions across the sequence:

| Sequence Position | Function | Example Focus |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Early Questions** | Identify the action and bring awareness to the behavioral mechanism. | “Do I take on more than I can handle to feel useful?” |
| **Middle Questions** | Expose what is missing or misperceived. | “Am I assuming others can’t manage without me?” |
| **Later Questions** | Challenge forward movement by contrasting intent and outcome. | “Does taking responsibility for everyone keep me from solving my own problems?” |

The sequence should build cognitive tension through logic rather than emotional intensity and conclude with **clarity and direction** rather than catharsis.

## **3. Ensuring Flow and Momentum**

After drafting, practitioners should review the sequence aloud.  
A compliant ELI builds **momentum through reasoning**, not repetition.  
Each question should move closer to a definable cognitive endpoint — the point at which the client can see both the error in logic and the viable behavioral alternative.
